The Hong Kong information war in Malaysia

How did ‘fake news’ on the Hong Kong protests arrive on our shores?

( Published on Dec 13, 2020 )

Context

This is the first investigation in Malaysia to look at how online disinformation campaigns originated from outside of the country were brought into our shores and spread within our online community during the anti-government protests in Hong Kong in 2019 and early 2020.

The series of street protests that started in June 2019 were triggered by a controversial bill that would have allowed extradition of fugitives to mainland China. As peaceful marches turned into violent confrontations between the protesters and police, a parallel information war erupted between the protesters and the pro-Hong Kong establishment faction in cyberspace.

Unlike the protests which were largely contained within Hong Kong, the information war quickly escalated into a disinformation race that involved China’s state media and other anti-Chinese Communist Party (CCP) forces. Their campaigns have generated a huge amount of false information that were spread to different countries including Malaysia triggering heated disputes among Malaysians both online and offline.

To further understand how such disinformation was brought into the public space of Malaysian online communities, who are the main creators and “importers” of such material and how far is the reach, we collected 11,482 posts from 384 Malaysian public Facebook pages published between June 2019 to March 2020 that contain keywords related to the protests. We then pored over all posts to identify false or misleading content and trace their origin and distribution paths.

Disinformation is false information that is deliberately created or disseminated with the express purpose to cause harm. Producers of disinformation typically have political, financial, psychological or social motivations. - First Draft

What we found

Almost all of the misinformation that we found was communicated in Chinese language in either traditional and simplified characters. Out of the 11,482 posts on Facebook that we collect, only around 10 percent of 1,096 posts are in English or Malay. Most of those posts are ordinary news reports about the protests without strong bias.

This is not surprising as the development of Hong Kong politics is always an issue that many Chinese Malaysians care about. Most of the detailed media coverage and materials including misinformation about the protests were also communicated in Chinese language by the media and other content creators. However, this observation should not be used to dismiss the possibility that there are disinformation campaigns that target non-Chinese audiences in Malaysia as our methodology has several limitations which you can read about them in the bottom of this report.

The disinformation ‘importers’: local Chinese media

Chinese language mainstream media is one of the main ‘importers’ of disinformation originated from China’s state media and other pro-CCP Hong Kong media.

We found many news reports about the protests published by local Chinese language mainstream media were taken in whole from foreign media including those that are known to be biased and hyper-partisan. Although specific terms and grammar used in those ‘copy-paste’ are clearly native to China, Taiwan or Hong Kong, no editing or localization was done to them. A small number of those ‘copy-paste’ reports did not even credit the original publisher but a simple search on Google would reveal the original reports.

By republishing these disinformation, local mainstream media not just lent credibility to them but amplified them to local audiences through local Facebook pages that like to share reports from local media with their followers. Some of the biggest Facebook pages in the dataset that we collected have hundreds thousands of followers, enabling the disinformation to reach potentially millions of Malaysians in a matter of hours.

We have picked four cases from our investigation to illustrate the role of local Chinese media in the disinformation campaign.

Case #1

A dubious story started by China’s state-owned media Global Times that claimed protesters threw Molotov cocktails at school busses was distributed to Malaysian audiences through local major Chinese dailies.

  • 2019 12 Nov

    China

    The Chinese version of Global Times, a news outlet under the Chinese Communist Party’s (CCP) mouthpiece, published an article on Nov 12, 2019 titled “Save the children! After rioters intimidate police’s kids and attack a school bus, we talk to mothers in Hong Kong”. (https://perma.cc/AP5P-BZ8R)

  • The article claimed that the Hong Kong police had confirmed in a press conference a day before that protesters had thrown Molotov cocktails in front of a school bus.

  • It posted two photos showing two different incidents of fire, allegedly caused by Molotov cocktails from the protesters, burning near a school bus and a public bus. However the article did not state that the school busses were the target of the Molotov cocktails attack.

  • The article has raised doubts as none of the interviewees have their full name or photo published. The article also failed to provide any details on the two photos showing fire burning near busses.

  • 2019 12 Nov

    China

    When the same article was published on the English version of Global Times (https://perma.cc/V9J9-LMZS) later that day, several important information were changed to assert that protesters were targeting school kids. The title has been changed to “Petrified HK parents rush to send kids to safety as rioters wreak havoc on school”.

    The first two summary points stated that “Rioters have targeted innocent school children in Hong Kong” and “They threw a Molotov cocktail at a school bus on Monday morning…”.

  • 2019 12 Nov

    China

    This story was quickly republished on various media outlets, social media platforms and online news hubs in China including state-run broadcaster GCTN (https://perma.cc/4ZM6-XEG2) and Sina News, one of the largest digital news hubs in China owned by Sina Corporation which also owns the micro-blogging platform Weibo.

  • 2019 12 Nov

    China

    Sina News (https://perma.cc/44K7-XM5E) published the article from Global Times’ Chinese version with a new title that read “Rioters intimidate children of police officers and attack school buses. Hong Kong mothers: please save the children” (暴徒恐吓警察子女袭击校车 香港妈妈：救救孩子).

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    The next day, almost all major Chinese dailies in Malaysia republished the story including Sin Chew Daily, Nanyang Siang Pau, Oriental Daily News and See Hua Daily News in Sarawak.

    In its Facebook page that had over 1.6 million likes then, Sin Chew Daily posted the story (https://perma.cc/6BX6-GPVH) and gave it a sensational title that read “Children's lives hang by a thread! Molotov cocktails thrown in front of Hong Kong school bus” (小朋友命悬一线！香港校车前 被丢汽油弹). The status accompanying the post read “Even kids were not spared 😨😨 “ (小朋友也不放过😨😨 ). The post’s image was created by joining side by side the two photos of the different incidents showing fire burning near busses.

  • The Facebook post quickly attracted comments that questioned the sources and the images of the story. According to the post’s edit history, the status, image and title of the post were all updated 83 minutes after it was posted. The new status read “Police press conference video inside” and the new image was a collage of the two photos showing fire near busses and a screenshot of CGTN video featuring a police spokesperson claiming a fire was started in front of a school bus.

  • 2019 14 Nov

    Malaysia

    On the following day, Sin Chew Daily posted the same story again with an apology (https://perma.cc/J44F-99UG). It clarified that the police only confirmed that the Molotov cocktail was burning in front of the school bus but did not say that the school bus was attacked by protesters.

  • According to Crowdtangle, a social media analytics tool, the first Facebook post which was later updated has attracted almost 4,000 interactions on Facebook including over 1,900 shares and 1,500 reactions. However, the second post with apology only recorded one third of the first post’s interactions with 184 shares and about 600 reactions.

  • Although Sin Chew Daily updated the first post in less than two hours and issued an apology the next day, the earlier misleading version has reached more Malaysian audiences beyond Sin Chew’s Facebook followers through other distributors.

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    One of them is a Facebook page called 槟城人 (Penang Lang) which means Penangites in Hokkien. It republished the earlier version of the story on its own website penanglang.com (https://perma.cc/AWX9-TA3C) and shared it on its Facebook page (https://perma.cc/6XQQ-72UN) which has over 430,000 likes.

  • 2019 15 Dec

    Malaysia

    Data from Crowdtangle showed that the webpage on penanglang.com has been further shared 101 times on Facebook including in a public group called Komuniti Kuantan ，(https://perma.cc/89GC-Q9RC) that has over 200,000 members.

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    Sin Chew Daily is not the only “importer” of such foreign disinformation. Nanyang Siang Pau copy-pasted the whole article from Sina News (https://perma.cc/5JYZ-NZHC) on its website (https://perma.cc/K7MA-P88Z) together with a slightly different title that read “Rioters attack school buses. Hong Kong mothers: please save the children” (示威者袭击校车 香港妈妈:救救孩子).

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    It was then posted on Nanyang Siang Pau’s Facebook page (https://perma.cc/YF84-AQSB) without the title but with the status “Please protect the children” (请保护孩子~) and one of the two photos showing fire burning in front of a school bus. According to the edit history, the status was updated two days later to “Chaotic” (乱象横生~).

  • 2019 14 Nov

    Malaysia

    This post was further shared 390 times including on a public group called “Ipoh chit-chat station”(https://perma.cc/F9EH-B2U2) (ipoh 怡保吹水站) that has over 300,000 members. Many shared the post with messages condemning the protesters.

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    See Hua Daily News, the Chinese daily with the highest circulation in East Malaysia, published a big part of the Global Times article on its website (https://perma.cc/G5BM-CVN4) with its own title “Hong Kong rioters throw Molotov cocktails at school bus. Poor children are scared to death” (港暴徒向校车丢汽油弹 可怜孩子吓到失魂). The article did not attribute the content to Global Times or any other foreign media.

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    After it was posted to See Hua Daily News Facebook page (https://perma.cc/27JK-AK3Q) with the same title and a catchy status that read “Too much!” (太over了), the story was shared 630 times.

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    Oriental Daily News, another Chinese daily under the same media group that owns See Hua Daily News, also published the story (https://perma.cc/ZS3A-FRK9) with the initial title “Hong Kong protesters throw Molotov cocktail. Smash school bus and broke window ” (港示威者掷汽油弹 砸校车打破车窗).

  • 2019 13 Nov

    Malaysia

    In its Facebook post (https://perma.cc/GES3-MGPF) , Oriental Daily News used an image that joined the two photos of fire burning near busses with a title in the image that read “Children’s school buses attacked. Angry parents: is Hong Kong still safe?” (子女校车遭袭击 家长怒：在香港还安全吗？). Status of the post read “There are children on the bus...” (车上有小孩的啊...).

  • Those sensational wordings and the image were changed hours later. The new title is “Hong Kong protesters throw Molotov cocktails. Police: burn in front of school bus” (港示威者掷汽油弹 警方：校车前燃烧). Image showing the fire near school busses was removed and status was updated to “School buses are not safe too...” (校车也不安全了...). The original version can still be viewed in posts that were shared by other users before the change took place.

  • 2019 13 & 14 Nov

    Malaysia

    The post was shared over 1,200 times but it is not sure how many of them were done before the changes. According to data from Crowdtangle, it was further shared to at least three public groups each with more than 10,000 members - Kepong chit-chat station (甲洞3817《吹水资讯站》) (https://perma.cc/A9SR-D4TV), Johor Bahru giant chit-chat station (新山吹水霸王站)(https://perma.cc/KQB9-8ZQB) and Reporters for the people (全民记者 News & Media)(https://perma.cc/63JA-8SNQ). The post continued to be shared to more people from those public groups.

  • Although all four dailies - Sin Chew, Nanyang, Oriental and See Hua - have published misleading titles in their earliest version of the story, only Sin Chew issued an apology and clarification.

Case #2

China Press published a story claiming that materials provided by the China embassy in Malaysia showed that Hong Kong protesters were trained by a ‘school’ named CANVAS which was established by CIA.

  • 2019 27 Aug

    Malaysia

    China Press published an article (https://perma.cc/G8YY-AVZN) on its website with the title “China embassy reveals that CIA established a training school to teach how to organize demonstrations” (中使馆披露 美情报机构设培训学校 手把手 教怎么搞游行).

  • It claimed that the China embassy in Malaysia has provided the media with a compilation of materials which includes the “facts and nature of the anti-extradition bill protests in Hong Kong” and interventions by foreign forces.

  • The article described in detail how protesters in Hong Kong were trained in “triggering riots, confronting the police and other non-violent movements” through a syllabus developed by the Center for Applied Nonviolent Action and Strategies (CANVAS). The article added that CANVAS received funding from CIA, according to information from an email published by whistleblowing website WikiLeaks.

  • 2019 9 Aug

    China

    Although China Press attributed the allegations to the embassy, a search online found that 19 out of the 21 paragraphs in the article were lifted in whole from an article (https://perma.cc/QX8V-JYYN) published nearly three weeks ago by CCTV, China’s state broadcaster. All the images used in the China Press article are the same as those in the CCTV article.

  • 2019 27 Aug

    Malaysia

    The China Press article was posted twice on the Chinese daily’s Facebook page (https://perma.cc/6A5V-ET6P) that has almost two millions followers. The posts were shared 42 times in total including four times on two other China Press regional Facebook pages - China Press Melaka (https://perma.cc/MRA5-K9XD) (over 68,000 followers) and China Press northern Malaysian (https://perma.cc/REY3-C4CQ) (over 104,000 followers).

  • According to CANVAS website (https://perma.cc/9PQ7-898Q), it is a non-profit organization based in Belgrade, Serbia that provides training to activists in the use of nonviolent resistance to promote human rights and democracy. The website listed some 50 countries from where activists they have worked with but Hong Kong and China were absent.

  • A check on the said email published by WikiLeaks (https://perma.cc/EW88-XJM5) did not find any strong evidence of CANVAS receiving funding from CIA.

  • China Press article also said the materials from the embassy claimed “the US has played an important role in the pro-democracy protests in Hong Kong in recent years by leading and training street protests”. It then cited a 2014 BBC report that claimed thousands of protesters in the Occupy Movement were trained in the Oslo Freedom Forum. The Occupy Movement (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_Hong_Kong_protests) is a pro-democracy movement in Hong Kong in 2014.

  • A check on the BBC report (https://perma.cc/L4VD-F9DQ) found that there’s a correction note stating that the report has been amended to clarify that the planning of the Occupy Movement was not done by foreigners and members of the movement organizer had not attended the forum. An earlier version of the BBC report can be viewed here (https://web.archive.org/web/20141022050950/https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-29708917).

  • Another false information that China Press lifted from the CCTV article originated from Ta Kung Pao, another China’s state-owned daily.

  • 2019 29 Jul

    Malaysia

    Ta Kung Pao claimed in an online article that its reporter had photographed (https://perma.cc/56TL-QDNS) “a foreigner commander using a social media app to inform the protesters about the movement of the police” during a protest. The CCTV article said the foreigner is a “technical mentor” of the protest, one of the operations taught by the CANVAS syllabus.

  • This claim has been fact-checked by AFP (https://perma.cc/E46D-6PU3) the next day after the Ta Kung Pao article was published. The foreigner is actually Kevin Roche, an employee of the New York Times newspaper, who was photographed communicating with other New York Times journalists covering the July 28, 2019 protest.

  • 2019 31 Jul

    Malaysia

    Our investigation also found this disinformation was republished by Nanyang Siang Pau. The article (https://perma.cc/2D9U-2HMS) featured a sensational title “Who is this American hiding beside the Hong Kong protesters?” (藏在香港示威者身边 这位美国人是何方神圣?).

Case #3

Nanyang Siang Pau republished a false information claiming that 23-year-old student activist Joshua Wong and his colleagues met with a diplomat with the US Consulate General in Hong Kong named Julie Eadeh to “plot” against Hong Kong. The title of the article read “Suspected American hands behind Hong Kong chaos exposed” (美乱港幕后黑手疑曝光).

  • 2019 8 Aug

    Malaysia

    In the short article (https://perma.cc/22N8-L3AP), the Chinese daily claimed that unnamed netizens witnessed the meeting occurred in the lobby of a Hong Kong hotel.

  • “According to the eyewitness, when they saw her ‘looking very American’, they acted very respectfully, as if they were meeting their big boss,” read the article with an alleged photo of the meeting.

  • 2019 8 Aug

    Malaysia

    After Nanyang Siang Pau posted the article on its Facebook page (https://perma.cc/V8HT-CWXF) with the status ”Hong Kong ‘separatists’ met with US embassy officials?!” (“港独”组织头目会美领馆人员？！), it was shared 144 times.

  • 2019 7 Aug

    Hong Kong

    The article, which only has three paragraphs, was attributed to ifeng.com, a news website under Phoenix New Media, a Hong Kong media conglomerate known for its pro-Beijing stance. The whole article, including the title and photo, was lifted from an article (https://perma.cc/Z5SF-J27Z) published on ifeng.com a day before.

  • 2019 7 Aug

    China

    Further check online revealed that ifeng.com republished the article from Chinanews.com (https://perma.cc/2BJV-MVZN), a news website under China News Service, a state-owned news agency in China.

  • 2019 7 Aug

    Hong Kong

    Chinanews.com took the story from Wen Wei Po (https://perma.cc/T9KZ-2J8R), a Hong Kong newspaper controlled by the CCP.

  • 2019 8 Aug

    Malaysia

    Nanyang Siang Pau is not the only local media that carried the story. See Hua Daily News (https://perma.cc/37TT-FHUZ), Kwong Wah Daily (https://perma.cc/AQ6E-6KFS) and Sin Chew Daily (https://perma.cc/MPW3-DRTN) also published the story on the same day as Nanyang but they have included Wong’s rebuttal.

  • 2019 7 Aug

    Hong Kong

    In a Facebook post (https://perma.cc/F764-6WBY) posted on the same day after Wen Wei Po made the allegation, Fong clarified that it was a usual meeting held after Eadeh met with Hong Kong legislators in the Legislative Council Complex. The meeting was to discuss the Hong Kong Human Rights and Democracy Act, which was later passed by the US congress three months later, and to urge the US to stop exporting tear gas and rubber bullets to Hong Kong police.

  • 2019 8 Aug

    China

    The same disinformation has been spreaded by other pro-Beijing websites and social media accounts including CCTV (https://perma.cc/7QMZ-H7X9), using it as one of the evidence to prove the conspiracy theory (https://perma.cc/9Q7P-GT85) that the US was secretly commanding and funding the protests in Hong Kong.

  • 2019 8 Aug

    Hong Kong

    Ta Kung Pao, another Hong Kong newspaper controlled by Beijing, described (https://perma.cc/C7HW-8ATB) Eadeh as “a mysterious, low-profile expert in subversion” with experience in warzones. It went further to disclose the background of her husband and names of her children.

  • 2019 7 Sep

    Malaysia

    The Chinese embassy in Malaysia also posted a video (https://perma.cc/SZ36-CUWS) on its Facebook page that used this disinformation to support the conspiracy theory. The 2-minute video, entitled “The ten chaos must be suppressed immediately”, showed the same photo of Wong meeting Eadeh to assert that the Hong Kong protests were “plotted, commanded and supported by Western powers”.

  • 2019 7 Sep

    Malaysia

    The Facebook post was shared 91 times. One user by the name of Jia Bao Yu (贾宝玉) (https://perma.cc/6ZBD-RR2U) has shared this post on more than 10 public groups, amplifying the disinformation to millions of Malaysian audiences. Some of those public groups have more than 100,000 members such as JB chit-chat station (https://perma.cc/FRD9-E234) (JB吹水站), “Bukit Mertajam chit-chat station” (https://perma.cc/S5HU-4PTK) (大山脚吹水站😉😉) and “Ipoh chit-chat station” (https://perma.cc/V27P-QTXB) (IPOH 大只广吹水交流区).

  • 2019 10 Aug

    China

    Radio Antarabangsa China (http://malay.cri.cn/) is another official channel of the Chinese government that distributed this disinformation to Malaysian audiences. Together with the message “traitors in Hong Kong riots” (pengkhianat dalam rusuhan di Hong Kong. #HK), it posted a video (https://perma.cc/NFT5-5JUJ) on its Facebook page on Aug 10, 2019 that featured the photo of Wong and Eadeh.

  • Radio Antarabangsa China is one of the online publications under the state-owned China Radio International. It is published in Malay language with the mission to “introduce China to the world”. Its Facebook page (https://perma.cc/CMF3-7G66) has accumulated more than 480,000 followers.

Case #4

Both See Hua Daily News and Kwong Wah Daily published the same report claiming that 7,500 Hong Kong residents have applied to migrate to Malaysia without quoting any official source. The report, which we believe originated from a Taiwanes news website, became viral after being posted on Facebook by Kwong Wah Daily. It was then ‘exported’ back to foreign media in Hong Kong and China.

  • 2019 12 Oct

    Malaysia

    Kwong Wah Daily published the article (https://perma.cc/C6TV-E2Q7) entitled “Spike to 4 times higher than previous years. 7,500 Hongkongers apply to migrate to Malaysia” (相较历年暴增4倍 7500港人申请移民大马).

  • The article claimed there are more Hong Kong residents applying to relocate to Malaysia through the Malaysia My Second Home (MM2H) program due to the ongoing protests in Hong Kong. It quoted a Taiwanese real estate agency which held a marketing event in Hong Kong.

  • It said that “According to statistics, the number of Hongkongers applying to migrate to Malaysia between January to October this year has achieved 7,500, four times higher than previous years.” It was not stated whether this figure is from the real estate agency or other official sources. The article also did not state whether the content is original reporting or reproduced from another news source.

  • 2019 12 Oct

    Malaysia

    It became viral after Kwong Wah Daily shared it on their Facebook page (https://perma.cc/2NY7-GK7V), receiving over 2,000 reactions and almost 1,000 shares.

  • 2019 12 Oct

    Malaysia

    See Hua Daily News also published the same article (https://perma.cc/3WC6-ZFBK) on the same day with exactly the same content but slightly different title. It is not clear which article was published earlier but both newspapers do not belong to the same media group.

  • 2019 14 Oct

    Malaysia

    See Hua Daily News posted the article on its Facebook page (https://perma.cc/E4PS-BHB8) two days later but only received 17 shares.

  • 2019 12 Oct

    Taiwan

    An online check found that the first half of the article was identical to a report (https://perma.cc/P45U-2EMK) published by Taiwanese news website ETtoday on the same morning. We believe ETtoday is the original author as its report has a byline of its reporter, photos with credit and the content is much longer than the Kwong Wah Daily’s version.

  • However, the ETtoday report did not provide the source of the 7,500 migration applications too.

  • 2019 20 Oct

    Taiwan

    The same report posted on ETtoday’s Facebook page (https://perma.cc/Q2VC-KM8M) failed to attract much attention with only two likes and no share at all.

  • 2019 12 Oct

    Hong Kong

    Part of Kwong Wah Daily’s article including the unverified figure was republished by Radio France Internationale (RFI) in a report (https://perma.cc/33Q2-QYZ9) in its Chinese version website. Citing Kwong Wah Daily, the RFI report highlighted the increasing migration of wealthy Hongkongers to other countries due to the protests and Hong Kong’s bleak outlook.

  • The RFI article was then republished by many other foreign Chinese-language websites including those in Hong Kong and mainland China. A search on Google using the first paragraph in the RFI article returned more than 100 results.

We noticed that this unverified information was used by both sides of the political divide to attack their rivals. Supporters of the establishment quoted the figure to highlight the damage done by the protests while the pro-protesters media including the New Tang Dynasty Television (https://perma.cc/L2RG-JGSG) and Epoch Times (https://perma.cc/8CYL-TX95) used it in reports claiming the Malaysian government has rejected the migration applications of Hong Kong police personnel.

The “7,500 applications from Hong Kongers between January to October 2019” claim is far from the official statistics under the MM2H program. According to the Ministry of Tourism, Arts and Culture (https://perma.cc/EH65-HGCE), the program received a total of 7,904 applications in 2019 from all over the world. Although county breakdown was not revealed, it is unlikely that 7,500 out of the 7,904 came from Hong Kong. Reuters also reported (https://perma.cc/34AJ-AR5M) that the program only received 251 applications from Hong Kong between January and the end of August 2019.

Although the official figures have been reported by both local and foreign media and more details could have been obtained from local authorities, both Kwong Wah Daily and See Hua Daily News failed to verify the information before republishing the story.

Malaysian Facebook dominated by pro-Beijing voice

We also discovered that the posts and the comments they attracted are overwhelmingly anti-protesters and pro-Beijing although both sides of the political divide - the police and the protesters - have resorted to excessive force and violence during the protests.

This could be caused by the scope of our investigation that only focused on Facebook. Most of the Facebook posts that we compiled are from local Chinese dailies which rely on information from mainstream media in Hong Kong and China. Some of the mainstream media in Hong Kong are clearly on the side of the establishment such as Wen Wei Po (文汇报) and Ta Kung Pao (大公报) while their counterparts in China are mostly state-owned mouthpieces like Global Times (环球时报) and CCTV. On the other hand, anti-China media like the various publications under the Falun Gong-affiliated Epoch Times (大纪元时报) that have been churning out disinformation attacking the CCP during the protests were not considered as ‘official’ information by local Chinese dailies.

It was widely reported (https://perma.cc/7Y5J-HXP7) that both sides generated and spread disinformation to sway public opinion but the platforms used by the protesters were very different from their rivals. They preferred messaging application Telegram that encrypts their messages and also protects their identity. It is hard for Information circulated on such platforms to go beyond local users and reach foreign media. Our investigation did not include any messaging applications. Read more about our methodology at the bottom.

Unverified or false information that can be construed as anti-Beijing or pro-protesters were more likely to be seen from Facebook pages not operated by local Chinese dailies. For example, NTV7 Mandarin News posted a segment of its tv news (https://perma.cc/8QHT-JCQB) on its Facebook page on Aug 20, 2019 about an armed attack by a man against three pro-democracy youths.

Citing unnamed sources, the report claimed that the attacker, a middle-age man, spoke with a mainland Chinese accent. It was unclear where this information came from as our online search could not find any Hong Kong media (https://perma.cc/K8MH-6VS5) that mentioned the accent. The attacker was later arrested and identified as a Hong Kong resident (https://perma.cc/8KGP-5X4L) who is pro-Beijing.

NTV7 Mandarin News was terminated and merged with 8TV Mandarin News this June.

Hyper-partisan Facebook pages join the skirmish

Our investigation has identified more than a dozen of popular public Facebook pages run by managers based in Malaysia that have been sharing misinformation about the protests. Through their highly partisan posts that include false and misleading information, the pages made it clear which side they were on. Videos, articles and news reports with sensationalizing and exaggerating headlines were shared by both pro- and anti-protesters pages. Such content often attracted huge responses from users who shared the same view.

However, it should be stressed that we found no evidence that indicates these pages were intentionally created to spread misinformation or propaganda for either side of the protests. Many of them have been active for several years and have built considerable following before the protests erupted.

Most of them can be categorized into three groups - town/state based communities like “I’m from Penang” (我来自槟城) (https://perma.cc/4ZZJ-HTBT), supporters of local political parties like “We Fully Support P.R- D.A.P 我们全力支持民行” (https://perma.cc/Y36K-H3DV), and news information sharing pages like “FBT News 新闻最前线” (https://perma.cc/8ERU-H634).

Further analysis of the content posted and shared by these pages found that many of them belong to networks of pages that amplified their messages by simultaneously posting the same content across different pages and cross-sharing those content. This could mean those pages were managed by the same people but we could not confirm this as Facebook only reveals the location of the page managers but not their identities and our investigation is only limited to posts on the Hong Kong protests.

One of those networks identified in our investigation consists of three Facebook pages - “World news” (https://perma.cc/8ERU-H634) (天下无所不闻), “I can’t find any reason to support BN” (https://perma.cc/5J7K-VWGE) (我真的找不到支持国阵的理由) and “Return my justice, support Anwar!“ (https://perma.cc/HV9B-YFPX) (还我公正，力挺安华！We Support Anwar！). They have a combined number of almost 60,000 likes.

They have cross-shared or published some 20 posts with identical content that was pro-Beijing or anti-protesters. Most of those posts used exaggerated or sensational language with false information. For example, one post about Hong Kong police searching for protesters that vandalised the legislative council building has a message that read “Ten thugs have been convicted! Hong Kong begins a citywide search! Foreign ministry denounces UK! Declare war if more interference!” (10名暴徒罪證已定！香港開始全城搜捕！外交部痛斥英國！再干涉就開戰！). Another post about US House speaker Nancy Pelosi’s statement that supports the protesters read “The hand behind Hong Kong protests is caught! China will not spare this 79-year-old American lady!” (香港幕後者，揪出來了！這個79歲美國女人，中國這次絕不饒過。).

Both posts are not available on Facebook anymore. The reasons are not clear but they are available in our database [database link] that we have made public.

Almost all their common posts were articles or videos published on Qiqu.live, a content farm targeting Malaysian audiences. This pattern was also found in other networks of pages. They consistently posted articles and videos related to the protests from the same content farm.

Content farms or content mills refer to websites that publish a large amount of low-quality content often with sensational titles to lure clicks and views with the purpose to earn advertising revenue or sway public opinions. Since their sole purpose is to attract attention, their content often contains misleading and false information.

According to The Reporter, an award-winning investigative journalism website based in Taiwan, a network of hundreds of content farms (https://perma.cc/YQK3-TUZP) targeting Chinese speaking audiences in Asia was run from Malaysia. The websites in this network include those with “Qiqu” in their domain names like Qiqu.live and Qiqu.pro and those with “KanWatch” like KanWatch.com, KanWatch.me and KanWatch.site.

A deeper look at Qiqu found that many of the videos it published on the China-US geopolitics originated from pro-Beijing Youtube channels like 时政焦点 (https://perma.cc/7X2N-NQPL), 中国新观察 (https://perma.cc/T4KX-EHWY) and 时政热点 (https://perma.cc/4SBM-JC53).

  • Another network of pages that distributed pro-Beijing disinformation were centered around “Being overseas” (https://perma.cc/FNX5-L7GM) (人在海外) which has and eight managers - six located in Malaysia and two in Singapore - and has close to 140,000 likes.

  • 2019 11 Jun

    Malaysia

    During the early stage of the protests, one of its posts (https://perma.cc/7D4S-9ERN) claimed that protesters have planned several strategies including the use of Molotov cocktails, explosives made of lithium-ion batteries and mini crossbows to charge the legislative council building on Jun 12, 2019 in order to stop the extradition bill from being passed.

  • 2019 11 Jun

    Hong Kong

    A check online showed that the content came from an unverified report (https://perma.cc/5V4Q-GUS5) published by the CCP-controlled Wen Wei Po on the same day. It based the information on the discussion in a protesters’ Telegram group.

  • 2019 11 Jun

    Malaysia

    Although the post received only 11 shares, the information was further amplified by other pages including "草根全力支持卖华贼下台"(https://perma.cc/A966-ZLSP) (Grassroot fully support Chinese traitors to resign) and "我们全力支持希望联盟" (https://perma.cc/677D-KPLZ) (We fully support Pakatan Harapan) that consistently shared posts from “Being overseas” during the protests.

  • The information turned out to be inaccurate as none mentioned weapons was seen during the protest on June 12 (https://perma.cc/ER79-2QBC). Instead they hurled umbrellas, traffic cones, water bottles and bricks that they dug up on the spot at police. Police responded with pepper spray, tear gas, bean bag bullets and rubber bullets.

  • 2019 14 Oct

    Malaysia

    The page shared a 3-min video (https://perma.cc/58UR-UYH8) that compiled footage of escalating violence by the protesters and called for support for Hong Kong police.

  • The video is one of the many misinformation shared by this page from another Facebook page “幫港出聲 Silent Majority” (https://perma.cc/7SFU-ASKB), a popular pro-Hong Kong establishment group that caters for Hong Kong audience. It has been found by Hong Kong fact-checking initiative Kaoyim Media (https://perma.cc/KN65-NWK5) (求驗傳媒) to be creating unfounded or misleading information related to the protests.

Our investigation also identified Facebook pages that distributed pro-protesters misinformation although the number is much lower than those in the opposite camp. Most of them were set up as pro-Pakatan Harapan/Rakyat pages.

  • “Rakyat Rise to Change the Regime” (https://perma.cc/7A3Q-REMR) (全民起義 改朝換代) was one of the most active and popular pro-protesters pages run by managers based in Malaysia.

  • It consistently provided pro-protesters disinformation to its 60,000 followers during the peak of the protests from foreign media especially Youtube channels affiliated with Epoch Times, a huge multi-platform media network backed by the anti-Beijing spiritual movement Falun Gong.

  • The channels include Epoch Times Hong Kong (https://perma.cc/5FY2-233R) (香港大紀元新唐人聯合新聞頻道), New Tang Dynasty Television (https://perma.cc/8DQ5-8LKQ) (新唐人電視臺), Crossroads of the World (https://perma.cc/WE8R-BWYJ) (世界的十字路口 唐浩) and Dayu Show (https://perma.cc/JQP5-DC4V) (新聞拍案驚奇 大宇).

  • Epoch Times Hong Kong, which has over 400,000 subscribers, was warned and temporarily suspended by Youtube in early November during the US presidential election but the reason was not known.

  • Started in March 2019, Dayu Show quickly accumulated over 575,000 subscribers in just 20 months. The show’s only host Lee Da Yu, was a broadcast journalist with NTDTV before joining the show.

Disinformation from the Chinese Embassy in Malaysia

As the top official representative of the China government in Malaysia, the Chinese Embassy was also found to be spreading disinformation through its official Facebook page.

Although the page only had a moderate following with less than 29,000 likes when the protests started in June 2019, the number rose to almost 47,000 in March 2020, a 60 percent growth in nine months, according to Crowdtangle data.

Serving as an official source of information, local media has been citing information posted by the Facebook page in their news report and treating them as official statements. Hence any information posted on the page has the potential to go beyond its followers and reach many more Malaysian audiences through the amplification of local media.

Most of the information posted on the page is about Chinese history, culture and the achievements of the Chinese government. However, we managed to identify at least videos shared by the page with misleading or unverified conspiracy theories that the protests in Hong Kong were funded and plotted by the west.

  • 2019 5 Aug

    Malaysia

    The official Facebook page of the Chinese Embassy in Malaysia shared a Youtube video (https://perma.cc/8RYT-H473) in English language with the message “The riots in #HongKong clearly require orchestration, leadership and most importantly, money. So who is the force behind the scenes? It’s time to unveil the true nature of #NationalEndowmentforDemocracy (#NED) in this video.” The post was shared 205 times.

  • It propagated the conspiracy theory that the protests in Hong Kong, both in 2014 and 2019, were funded by the US through the National Endowment for Democracy (NED), a US agency funded by the US Congress to promote democracy abroad. The video claimed that NED is an extension of the CIA with the purpose to destabilize rival countries.

  • 2019 30 Jul

    China

    The original video (https://perma.cc/W2HW-X7SL) was published by Beijing Review, a state-owned English news magazine, on its Youtube channel with the title “U.S. Fingerprints All Over Hong Kong Violence”.

  • Although pro-democracy groups in Hong Kong have been receiving funding from NED for quite some time, there's no clear evidence to prove such funding is used to fund the protests.

  • 2019 12 Jun

    Malaysia

    The Facebook page shared another Youtube video (https://perma.cc/HZ2T-WURX) that was packed with conspiracy theories.

  • Entitled “Deconstructing how the ‘color revolution’ by US destabilised a country in 12 steps and analysis of the Occupy Central Movement, Ukraine and Arab Spring” (解构美国如何‘颜色革命’12步搞乱一个国家，浅析占中事件乌克兰阿拉伯之春), the 26-minute video claimed that the same modus operandi was repeated in the 2014 Hong Kong protests.

  • 2016 30 Oct

    Unknown

    Although the embassy described the video as an expose by Hong Kong media, the video (https://perma.cc/3DLU-UDKR) was uploaded to Youtube nearly three years ago by an unknown channel called “Ciao chef Lev” with only 199 subscribers. It was first published by a Hong Kong church.

  • It repeated the allegation that the NED was funding and supporting the protesters to trigger a ‘color revolution’ in Hong Kong similar to the uprising in Arab countries and Ukraine which are part of a global conspiracy by Freemason forces.

  • “Freemasons will use Hong Kong as an opportunity to beat China, forcing China to participate in the third world war. The number of people hurt would increase by hundreds of times,” the video concluded.

Methodology

This project used Zanroo (https://enterprise.zanroo.com/), a paid social listening and monitoring tool, to collect posts from Malaysian public Facebook pages in all three languages (English, Malay and Chinese) based on a set of keywords related to the Hong Kong protests posted from 1 June 2019 to 31 March 2020. The resulting dataset consists of 11,482 posts from 384 pages together with other details of each post including number of engagements, date and time of posting, link and media shared, and number of followers. However, there is a small number of posts unrelated to Hong Kong protests but mistakenly included in the dataset because they share the same keywords.

Due to limited resources, our investigation focuses on Facebook as it is the most popular social media platform used by Malaysians. We only cover Facebook pages that are categorized as Malaysian pages based on various criteria including their page description, location of the managers, contact number and related websites. Such categorization is done by the team at Zanroo.

Our team then manually inspected each post in the dataset which received 20 and more engagements. Engagement is the total of all reactions (like, love, care, wow, haha, angry, sad), comments and shares received by a post. For each post, we identified if it contains misinformation or other relevant information. If it shares a post from another source, a Youtube video or links to other web pages, we would track and inspect their content for any misinformation.

For web pages or Youtube videos that are linked from those Facebook pages, we were able to track how many engagements they have received on different social media platforms and identify which public Facebook pages have also shared them using the CrowdTangle Link Checker (https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/crowdtangle-link-checker/klakndphagmmfkpelfkgjbkimjihpmkh), a Chrome extension.

Limitations

Due to Facebook’s privacy terms, we can only extract posts from public pages. Access to all posts from personal users are restricted by Facebook. Although we can view the comments posted by personal users on public pages, we can’t trace their profiles. Hence we have excluded all comments by personal users from our investigations.

The dataset of posts by Malaysian public Facebook pages that Zanroo built for us is not exhaustive as Zanroo depends on various signals shown on a Facebook page to determine if the page should be categorized as a Malaysian page. For example, a page that is followed by mostly Malaysian users but does not show any affiliation to Malaysia in its description, managers’ location, contact number or websites might not be categorized as a Malaysian page and included in the dataset.
